# Intellectual Property Law Reference

Patents, copyrights, trademarks, and trade secrets with registration procedures and infringement analysis.

**Sources:** USPTO, Copyright Office, Cornell LII (accessed March 2026)

---

## IP Law Basics Every Agent Must Know

**Trademark (Lanham Act):**
- Rights arise from **use**, not registration; registration creates legal presumption of validity and constructive nationwide notice
- Core test: **likelihood of confusion** (eight factors, no single factor dispositive)
- Famous marks get **dilution protection** even without confusion
- Registration lasts **10 years**, renewable indefinitely with continued use
- Always conduct trademark clearance search before launch

**Copyright:**
- Arises **automatically** upon creation of original work fixed in tangible medium
- Registration required before suing for **statutory damages and attorney's fees**
- Duration: Life + 70 years (individual); 95 years from publication (work for hire)

**Patent:**
- **No common law rights** — rights arise only from USPTO grant
- Utility patents: **20 years from filing** date
- Requires novelty, non-obviousness, utility
- Must file before public disclosure in most countries (one-year grace period in US)

**Trade Secrets:**
- Protects confidential business information with reasonable measures to maintain secrecy
- Federal civil cause of action under **DTSA** (Defend Trade Secrets Act, 2016, 18 U.S.C. §§1836-1839)
- State law (Uniform Trade Secrets Act, adopted by most states) also applies
- No registration; protection lasts as long as secrecy is maintained

## Trademarks — Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. Chapter 22)

**Sources:**
- Cornell LII: https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/15/chapter-22
- USPTO Trademark Statutes: https://www.uspto.gov/sites/default/files/trademarks/law/Trademark_Statutes.pdf

### Structure

| Subchapter | Content |
|------------|---------|
| I (§§1051-1072) | Principal Register — registration, examination, publication, opposition |
| II (§§1091-1096) | Supplemental Register — descriptive marks not yet registrable on Principal Register |
| III (§§1111-1129) | General provisions — constructive notice, damages, counterfeiting, cyberpiracy |
| IV (§§1141-1141n) | Madrid Protocol — international registration through WIPO |

### Registration Process

**Application bases:**
- Use in commerce (§1(a)): Mark already in use
- Intent to use (§1(b)): Mark not yet in use but with bona fide intent

**Examination:** USPTO examines for distinctiveness; generic and merely descriptive marks refused unless acquired secondary meaning

**Publication:** Published in Official Gazette; 30-day opposition period

**Incontestability:** After 5 years of continuous use, file Section 15 declaration — significant evidentiary benefit in litigation

**Distinctiveness hierarchy (most to least protectable):**
1. Fanciful (coined words — KODAK, XEROX)
2. Arbitrary (real word applied to unrelated goods — APPLE for computers)
3. Suggestive (hints at quality but requires imagination — COPPERTONE)
4. Descriptive (requires secondary meaning to be protected)
5. Generic (never protectable)

### Section 32 — Infringement of Registered Marks (§1114)
**Elements plaintiff must prove:**
1. Valid registered mark
2. Use in commerce
3. Likelihood of confusion, mistake, or deception

**Likelihood of Confusion Factors** (varies by circuit — Polaroid, Sleekcraft, DuPont):
- Strength of the mark
- Similarity of the marks
- Proximity of goods/services
- Channels of trade
- Consumer sophistication
- Actual confusion evidence
- Bridging the gap likelihood
- Quality of defendant's goods/services

**Remedies:** Defendant's profits, plaintiff's damages, costs, attorney's fees (exceptional cases), enhanced damages for willful infringement, destruction of infringing goods

### Section 43(a) — Unfair Competition (§1125(a))
- Extends protection to **unregistered marks** and trade dress
- Covers false designation of origin (passing off) and false advertising
- False advertising requires: false/misleading statement, materiality, interstate commerce, injury

### Section 43(c) — Dilution (§1125(c))
- Protects **famous marks** from uses that "blur" or "tarnish" the mark even without confusion
- Fame requires widespread recognition by general consuming public of the U.S.

### Section 43(d) — Cybersquatting / ACPA (§1125(d))
- Prohibits bad-faith registration of domains identical or confusingly similar to distinctive/famous marks
- In rem jurisdiction allows suit against the domain name itself
- Remedies: cancellation/transfer of domain, damages ($1,000-$100,000/domain)

### Common Questions
- **Do I need to register?** No — common law rights arise from use. But registration provides nationwide priority, constructive notice, and stronger remedies
- **Can I use a competitor's trademark in comparative ads?** Yes if truthful and not likely to cause confusion (nominative fair use)
- **Someone registered my brand name as a domain — what do I do?** File UDRP arbitration through ICANN (faster/cheaper), or sue under ACPA
- **What's the difference between TM, SM, and ®?** TM/SM = unregistered claim; ® = federally registered mark only (criminal penalties for false use of ®)
- **Can trade dress be protected?** Yes under §43(a) if distinctive and non-functional (product packaging, restaurant décor)

### Enforcement
- **USPTO:** Registration only; no private rights enforcement
- **Federal courts:** Civil enforcement of private trademark rights
- **CBP:** Can seize counterfeit goods at border once mark is recorded

## Copyrights (17 U.S.C.)

**Source:** https://www.copyright.gov/title17/
**Full PDF:** https://www.copyright.gov/title17/title17.pdf

### What Is Copyrightable
- Original works of authorship fixed in tangible medium
- Categories: Literary works, musical works, dramatic works, choreography, pictorial/graphic/sculptural works, motion pictures, sound recordings, architectural works, software (literary works)
- **Not copyrightable:** Ideas, facts, methods, systems, concepts (idea/expression dichotomy — §102(b))

### Duration
- Individual: Life + 70 years
- Work for hire / anonymous / pseudonymous: 95 years from publication or 120 years from creation (whichever shorter)

### Registration
- Automatic upon creation
- **Registration required** before suing for statutory damages and attorney's fees
- Must register before infringement begins (or within 3 months of publication) for enhanced remedies

### Fair Use (§107) — Four Factors
1. **Purpose and character** of use (commercial vs. nonprofit; transformative vs. reproductive)
2. **Nature** of copyrighted work (factual vs. creative)
3. **Amount and substantiality** of portion used
4. **Effect on market** for the copyrighted work (most important factor)

**Transformative use** is heavily weighted in fair use analysis (*Campbell v. Acuff-Rose Music*, 1994; *Andy Warhol Foundation v. Goldsmith*, 2023)

### DMCA §512 — Safe Harbor for Online Platforms
Platforms avoid liability for user-uploaded infringing content if they:
1. Have a registered DMCA agent with the Copyright Office
2. Implement and communicate a repeat infringer policy
3. Respond expeditiously to valid takedown notices
4. Have no "red flag" knowledge of infringement
5. Receive no direct financial benefit from infringement they have right/ability to control

**Takedown notice requirements (§512(c)(3)):** Identification of work, identification of infringing material with URL, statement of good faith belief, statement of accuracy under penalty of perjury, physical/electronic signature

### DMCA §1201 — Anti-Circumvention
- Prohibits circumvention of technical protection measures (TPMs/DRM)
- Separate from copyright infringement — violations occur even without underlying infringement
- Exceptions: security research, encryption research, reverse engineering for interoperability

### Statutory Damages (§504)
- General: $750 - $30,000 per work infringed
- Willful infringement: up to $150,000 per work
- Innocent infringement: as low as $200 per work

### Common Questions
- **Do I own work created by my employees?** Yes — work for hire doctrine; employer is author of works made within scope of employment
- **Do I own work created by contractors?** Not automatically — must have written work-for-hire agreement for qualifying categories, or assignment clause
- **Can I use a meme/photo I found online?** Copyright attaches automatically; fair use analysis required
- **Does AI-generated content get copyright protection?** Copyright Office position (2023): requires human authorship; purely AI-generated works not protectable

## Patents (35 U.S.C.)

**Source:** https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/35
**USPTO APIs:** https://developer.uspto.gov

### Types of Patents

| Type | What It Covers | Term |
|------|---------------|------|
| **Utility** | Machines, processes, compositions of matter, improvements | 20 years from filing |
| **Design** | Ornamental design of functional item | 15 years from grant |
| **Plant** | Asexually reproduced plant varieties | 20 years from filing |

### Patentability Requirements (§§101-103)

**§101 — Patent-eligible subject matter:**
- Utility, plant, or design claims
- NOT eligible: Laws of nature, natural phenomena, abstract ideas (*Alice Corp. v. CLS Bank*, 2014 — software patent eligibility test; *Mayo Collaborative Services v. Prometheus*, 2012)
- Software patents possible if claim is directed to specific technological implementation

**§102 — Novelty:**
- No prior art (patents, publications, public use, public disclosure) before earliest effective filing date
- US has one-year grace period for inventor's own disclosures

**§103 — Non-obviousness:**
- Would not have been obvious to a person of ordinary skill in the art at the time of invention
- Graham v. John Deere factors: scope/content of prior art, differences, level of ordinary skill, secondary considerations

**§112 — Written Description and Enablement:**
- Specification must describe the invention fully enough that a person of ordinary skill could make and use it (enablement)
- Claims must be supported by written description

### Infringement (§271)

**Direct infringement:** Making, using, selling, offering for sale, or importing a patented invention

**Induced infringement:** Actively encouraging another to infringe (must know of patent)

**Contributory infringement:** Selling component especially made for use in infringement with no substantial non-infringing uses

### Remedies (§§283-285)
- **Damages:** At least a reasonable royalty; lost profits if plaintiff can prove them
- **Enhanced damages:** Up to 3x for willful infringement
- **Injunctive relief:** No longer automatic; four-factor eBay test
- **Attorney fees:** Exceptional cases only (§285)

### PTAB Proceedings (Post-Grant Challenges)
- **Inter Partes Review (IPR, §§311-319):** Challenge patent validity based on prior art patents/publications; must be filed within 1 year of service of infringement complaint
- **Post-Grant Review (PGR, §§321-329):** Broader challenge on any invalidity ground; must be filed within 9 months of patent grant

### Common Questions
- **When must I file?** Before public disclosure in most countries; US has one-year grace period for your own disclosures
- **Can software be patented?** Yes if directed to specific technological implementation, not just abstract idea (*Alice* test)
- **Do I need to mark my product with the patent number?** Yes to recover damages for pre-lawsuit infringement; virtual marking on website acceptable
- **What is freedom to operate?** Analysis of whether a product/process infringes existing patents before commercialization

## Trade Secrets (Defend Trade Secrets Act, 18 U.S.C. §§1836-1839)

**Source:** https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/18/1836

### Definition (§1839(3))
- Financial, business, scientific, technical, or engineering information
- Owner took **reasonable measures to keep it secret**
- The information derives economic value from not being generally known

### What Qualifies
- Formulas, patterns, compilation, programs, device, method, technique, or process
- Examples: Algorithms, customer lists, pricing data, manufacturing processes, business strategies

### Misappropriation
- Acquisition through improper means (theft, bribery, espionage, breach of duty to maintain secrecy)
- Disclosure or use without consent

### Remedies
- Injunctive relief (including ex parte seizure)
- Damages (actual + unjust enrichment) or reasonable royalty
- Exemplary damages up to 2x for willful misappropriation
- Attorney's fees for willful misappropriation or bad-faith claims

### Reasonable Measures to Maintain Secrecy (Critical for Protection)
- NDAs with employees, contractors, and partners
- Access controls (physical and digital)
- Need-to-know policies
- Confidentiality markings on documents
- Employee training and exit interviews
- Non-compete agreements (enforceable in most states with limitations)

### Common Questions
- **Does it matter that a competitor independently developed the same thing?** No — trade secret protection does not prevent independent discovery (unlike patents)
- **Can we sue a former employee?** Yes, if they used or disclosed trade secrets in breach of their duty of confidentiality
- **What's the difference between DTSA and state trade secret law?** DTSA provides federal court jurisdiction; state UTSA still applies; can plead both
- **How long does protection last?** As long as the information remains secret — potentially indefinitely (unlike patents)
