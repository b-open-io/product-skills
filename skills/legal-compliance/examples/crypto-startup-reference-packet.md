# Crypto Startup Reference Packet

Attorney-facing research packet for US startup operations, stablecoins, token classification, custody, tax reporting, and selected state overlays.

**Prepared for:** Anthony legal agent workflows  
**Last verified:** July 14, 2026  
**Scope:** US federal law; Florida, Michigan, Delaware; New York as comparator only  
**Important:** This is research support, not legal advice.

## Executive Summary

- The current federal stablecoin **statute** is the **GENIUS Act**, enacted as **S.1582 / Pub. L. 119-27** on **July 18, 2025**. **Enacted ≠ fully effective:** under § 20, the Act and its amendments take effect on the earlier of 18 months after enactment (**January 18, 2027**) or 120 days after primary Federal payment stablecoin regulators issue any final implementing regulations. § 17’s securities/commodities carve-out for payment stablecoins of **permitted payment stablecoin issuers** tracks that schedule.
- The principal unresolved federal market-structure bill is the **Digital Asset Market Clarity Act (H.R. 3633)**, which passed the House on **July 17, 2025**, was reported in the Senate and placed on the Senate Legislative Calendar on **June 1, 2026** (Cal. No. 423), and is **not enacted** as of July 14, 2026.
- For startups, the recurring issue stack is still:
  - securities classification
  - stablecoin issuance and reserve design
  - FinCEN/MSB exposure
  - custody and exchange/intermediation risk
  - IRS broker reporting
  - sanctions controls
  - state money-transmission overlays
- Delaware remains a key entity and governance state, but not a substitute for federal crypto compliance.

## Stablecoin-Specific Section

### Core Federal Anchor

- **GENIUS Act**
  - Congress.gov identifies **S.1582** as having become **Public Law No. 119-27** on **July 18, 2025**.
  - White House materials describe GENIUS as creating the first federal regulatory system for payment stablecoins and emphasize 100% reserve backing with liquid assets and monthly public reserve disclosures.
  - **Operative timing:** § 20 effective date (earlier of Jan. 18, 2027 or 120 days after final implementing regs); § 3(b) three-year digital-asset-service-provider offer/sale transition; § 17 securities/commodities carve-out limited to payment stablecoins of **permitted payment stablecoin issuers** and subject to § 20.

### Main Questions Counsel Should Ask First

1. Is the product a “payment stablecoin” rather than a yield-bearing or investment-style instrument?
2. Who is the issuer, and what supervisory path is being contemplated?
3. Are reserves segregated, liquid, and redeemable on terms consistent with the statute and any implementing guidance?
4. Does the operating model involve custody, fiat conversion, transmission, or hosted-wallet intermediation?
5. Are freeze / seizure / burn or comparable lawful-order controls implicated by the product design?
6. Is the token being marketed as a payment instrument or drifting into investment language?

### Key Practical Overlay

- Even if the stablecoin is structured to avoid securities treatment, federal AML, sanctions, tax, and state money-transmission analyses remain live.

## Startup Operational Risk Section

### 1. Token Classification Risk

- Any token launch, sale, airdrop, or public distribution should start with a written Howey analysis.
- The SEC's April 2025 stablecoin statement is narrow and fact-specific; it does not immunize all “stablecoins.”

### 2. Custody / Intermediation Risk

- Hosted custody, wallet control, fiat conversions, or transaction intermediation increase:
  - FinCEN/MSB exposure
  - state money-transmission risk
  - securities / broker / ATS questions in some models

### 3. Marketing Risk

- Promoting appreciation, yield, or protocol-driven profit can materially worsen securities-law exposure.
- For stablecoins, investment-style language undermines the strongest non-security arguments.

### 4. Tax Reporting Risk

- IRS final regulations require brokers to report:
  - gross proceeds for transactions effected on or after **January 1, 2025**
  - basis on certain transactions effected on or after **January 1, 2026**
- Hosted-wallet and custodial-platform models should be reviewed carefully against broker definitions.

### 5. Sanctions Risk

- Treasury enforcement history shows that virtual-currency firms are expected to know who and where their customers are, and to prevent access by sanctioned parties or jurisdictions.

### 6. Entity-Structuring Risk

- Delaware can improve governance and liability architecture, but it does not solve federal token, AML, or sanctions issues.
- DAO or protocol-governance products still need a liability and entity analysis, especially where there is control, treasury management, or customer-facing services.

## Selected State Appendix

### Florida

- OFR maintains a fintech policy office expressly covering virtual currency and blockchain technology.
- Florida's money-transmitter framework remains the main state-law lens for crypto-adjacent operations.
- Best current framing: Florida is function-driven and regulator-active, but not a standalone virtual-currency licensing state like New York.

### Michigan

- DIFS administers Michigan's money transmission regime.
- Michigan publishes consumer and licensing materials touching crypto, but the state does not present a single mature crypto code comparable to BitLicense.
- Best current framing: Michigan is a money-transmission state with crypto relevance, but not with highly specific statutory tailoring.

### Delaware

- Delaware matters for:
  - LLC and alternative-entity structuring
  - freedom-of-contract governance
  - tokenized-cap table or stock-ledger architecture
- Delaware statutory materials expressly permit certain corporate records, including stock ledgers, to be kept on distributed electronic networks or databases if statutory conditions are met.

### New York Comparator

- BitLicense remains the clearest US state virtual-currency licensing regime.
- Use it as a comparator to pressure-test custody, transmission, exchange, and issuance functions.

## Key Cases and Enforcement Matters

### Priority Matters

- **SEC v. W.J. Howey Co.**
  - Foundational investment-contract test.
- **SEC v. Ripple Labs**
  - Important but fact-specific digital-asset decision; do not overread.
- **SEC v. Coinbase / Binance (historical / fact-specific)**
  - Past enforcement reference points for exchange, broker, staking, and token-listing theories.
  - Coinbase civil action: SEC announced **dismissal** on **February 27, 2025** (Press Release 2025-47). Do not present as a current live SEC enforcement template.
- **CFTC v. McDonnell**
  - Useful baseline for CFTC commodity framing in crypto.
- **CFTC v. Ooki DAO**
  - Important reference for DAO liability and regulated-activity exposure.
- **Treasury / OFAC / FinCEN actions against Bittrex and Binance**
  - High-signal compliance precedents for AML and sanctions expectations.

## Open Questions / Counsel-Only Issues

- When the GENIUS § 20 effective date triggers (final implementing regulations vs. January 18, 2027 outer date) and how far supervision, issuer pathways, and preemption develop in rules.
- Whether and when Congress will enact a durable market-structure bill such as CLARITY (still not law as of July 14, 2026).
- Exact boundary lines between software-only protocol authorship and regulated intermediation in specific factual patterns.
- How state regulators outside New York will modernize money-transmission regimes for stablecoins, hosted wallets, and protocol-linked payment rails.
- The extent to which tokenized-security structures produce new securities, security entitlements, or merely new recordkeeping layers under specific facts.
