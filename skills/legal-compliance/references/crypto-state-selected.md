# Selected State Crypto Law Notes

Startup-operations overlay for Florida, Michigan, Delaware, and New York.

**Last verified:** July 14, 2026  
**Scope:** only the states relevant to the current product-skills upgrade  
**Caution:** state digital-asset treatment is highly fact-specific and often turns on whether the business receives funds or virtual currency for transmission, holds customer assets, or merely develops software.

## What Matters

- **Florida** matters because OFR has an active fintech policy function and a money-transmitter framework that can capture crypto-adjacent activity on a functional basis.
- **Michigan** matters because DIFS administers a money transmission statute, but the digital-asset fit is not as clean or expressly modernized as New York's.
- **Delaware** matters less as a crypto-license state and more as an entity-formation, governance, and tokenized-recordkeeping state.
- **New York** remains the comparator outlier because BitLicense is still the clearest standalone virtual-currency licensing regime.

## Florida

### Governing Authority

- Florida Office of Financial Regulation, Money Transmitters page:
  - https://flofr.gov/divisions-offices/division-of-consumer-finance/money-transmitters
- Florida Office of Fintech Policy:
  - https://flofr.gov/divisions-offices/office-of-fintech-policy
- Florida Statutes, Chapter 560, Money Services Businesses:
  - https://www.leg.state.fl.us/Statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&Title=->Chapter+560&URL=0500-0599/0560/0560ContentsIndex.html
- OFR Blockchain Technology and Virtual Currency Whitepaper:
  - https://flofr.gov/docs/default-source/documents/ofr-blockchain-technology-and-virtual-currency-whitepaper.pdf

### Current Status as of July 14, 2026

- Florida does not have a separate BitLicense-style virtual-currency statute.
- OFR regulates money transmitters under Chapter 560 and maintains an Office of Fintech Policy that expressly covers virtual currency and blockchain technology.
- The OFR money-transmitters page says a Part II money-transmitter license authorizes transmission of currency, monetary value, or payment instruments, including via electronic transfer or the internet.
- OFR's whitepaper states that Florida state-chartered financial institutions were told in January 2022 that Florida financial-institutions codes do not prohibit them from offering virtual-currency services, including custody, if they conduct due diligence and risk assessment.

### Operational Implication for Startups

- A crypto or stablecoin business operating in or from Florida should expect a **functional** analysis:
  - Are you receiving money or value for transmission?
  - Are you holding customer assets?
  - Are you acting as an intermediary rather than selling your own software?
- Pure software or protocol development is generally a stronger fact pattern than custody, hosted-wallet, or transmission models.
- Bank partnerships or state-chartered financial-institution relationships may be easier to frame in Florida than in some other states, but that does not remove federal obligations.

### Unresolved or Contested Points

- Florida's statutes do not cleanly modernize every virtual-currency business model.
- Many crypto questions still collapse into fact-heavy analogies to money transmission, custody, or payment-instrument activity.
- Do not overstate Florida as a “crypto-safe harbor” state. The better description is “active regulator with fintech infrastructure but no standalone crypto code comparable to New York.”

## Michigan

### Governing Authority

- Michigan Money Transmission Services Act:
  - https://www.legislature.mi.gov/Laws/MCL?objectName=MCL-Act-250-of-2006
- Michigan MCL § 487.1004:
  - https://legislature.mi.gov/Laws/MCL?objectName=MCL-487-1004
- Michigan DIFS licensing / locator context:
  - https://www.michigan.gov/difs/difs-locator-faq

### Current Status as of July 14, 2026

- Michigan regulates money transmission through the Money Transmission Services Act administered by DIFS.
- The legislature site continues to publish Act 250 of 2006 and related sections.
- Michigan consumer and enforcement materials discuss cryptocurrency risk, kiosks, and money-transmission supervision, but the statute itself is not a modern crypto-code in the way New York's BitLicense rule is.

### Operational Implication for Startups

- Treat Michigan as a **money-transmission state with limited crypto-specific tailoring**.
- If the model involves custody, hosted wallets, customer funds, exchange intermediation, or fiat rails, DIFS licensing and money-transmission analysis usually requires analysis; document the answer rather than treating licensure as automatic.
- If the business is software-only, protocol-only, or self-custody tooling, the argument against licensure is stronger, but it should be documented carefully.

### Unresolved or Contested Points

- Michigan's statutes and public materials do not provide the same level of crypto-specific classification detail as New York.
- Avoid categorical claims that Michigan “does” or “does not” license all virtual-currency activity. The safer formulation is that the analysis remains fact-dependent under the money-transmission framework.

## Delaware

### Governing Authority

- Delaware LLC Act, 6 Del. C. ch. 18:
  - https://delcode.delaware.gov/title6/c018/sc01/index.html
- 6 Del. C. § 18-1101 freedom-of-contract policy:
  - https://delcode.delaware.gov/title6/c018/sc11/index.html
- Delaware General Corporation Law, 8 Del. C. § 201:
  - https://delcode.delaware.gov/title8/c001/sc06/
- Delaware stock ledger / distributed-ledger recordkeeping:
  - 8 Del. C. §§ 219, 224
  - https://www.delcode.delaware.gov/title8/Title8.pdf

### Current Status as of July 14, 2026

- Delaware does not provide a primary standalone crypto licensing regime comparable to New York.
- Delaware remains highly relevant for:
  - LLC and alternative-entity structuring
  - contractual flexibility
  - stock-transfer and recordkeeping rules
  - use of distributed electronic networks or databases for corporate records
- Title 8 permits corporate records, including stock ledgers, to be kept on electronic networks or databases, including distributed electronic networks or databases, so long as statutory conditions are met.

### Operational Implication for Startups

- Delaware is usually the leading state for:
  - issuer and operating-company formation
  - governance design
  - tokenized-equity or tokenized-recordkeeping structures
  - SPV / LLC / holding-company architecture
- For tokenized securities or other tokenized capital-stack products, Delaware entity law can complement Article 8 and federal securities analysis, but it does not replace it.

### Unresolved or Contested Points

- Delaware's corporate flexibility should not be confused with substantive federal permission to issue or sell tokens.
- A Delaware LLC or corporation does not solve Howey, broker-dealer, ATS, CFTC, FinCEN, or GENIUS issues by itself.

## New York Comparator

### Governing Authority

- NYDFS Virtual Currency Business Licensing:
  - https://www.dfs.ny.gov/virtual_currency_businesses

### Why It Matters

- NYDFS says a person engaging in virtual-currency business activity involving New York or a New York resident generally needs either a BitLicense or a New York banking charter with approval to conduct that activity.
- NYDFS lists the classic licensable buckets:
  - receiving or transmitting virtual currency
  - custody or control on behalf of others
  - buying and selling as a customer business
  - exchange services
  - controlling, administering, or issuing a virtual currency
- NYDFS also states that software development by itself does not require a BitLicense, while custody-heavy wallet services likely do.

### Comparator Takeaway

- New York remains the most explicit warning-state for crypto business-model analysis.
- Even when a company is not planning to serve New York, NYDFS categories are often useful as a stress-test for custody, transmission, exchange, and issuance issues.

## Practical State-Law Triage

Use this sequence:

1. Ask whether the startup touches customer assets, fiat rails, or hosted custody.
2. If yes, treat the model as presumptively in-scope for state money-transmission or virtual-currency licensing analysis (document the analysis; do not treat licensure as a foregone conclusion).
3. If no, document why the model is software, protocol, or self-custody only.
4. Use Delaware for entity and governance questions, not as a substitute for federal regulatory analysis.
5. Use New York as the “strictest comparator,” not the default operating assumption for Florida or Michigan.
