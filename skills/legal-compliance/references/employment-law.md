# Employment Law Reference

Deep dive into the four major federal employment statutes: Title VII, FLSA, ADA, and FMLA. With key cases, compliance checklists, and enforcement guidance.

**Sources:** EEOC, DOL Wage and Hour Division, ADA.gov (all accessed March 2026)

---

## Core Rules for Employment Law Agents

1. **15-employee threshold** governs Title VII and ADA; **50-employee threshold** governs FMLA; FLSA applies to nearly all employers in interstate commerce
2. **Protected classes under federal law:** Race, color, religion, sex (incl. pregnancy, sexual orientation per *Bostock*, gender identity), national origin (Title VII); age 40+ (ADEA); disability (ADA); genetic information (GINA)
3. **Exempt vs. non-exempt under FLSA:** Both a duties test AND a salary level test ($684/week minimum) must be satisfied — either alone is insufficient
4. **Reasonable accommodation** is an active ongoing obligation under ADA and Title VII (religion); not satisfied by a one-time denial
5. **Retaliation claims** often survive even when the underlying discrimination claim fails; anti-retaliation provisions exist in all four laws
6. **State law often provides greater protection** — California, New York, Illinois routinely have broader coverage thresholds, additional protected classes, longer SOL
7. **EEOC exhaustion is required** before filing Title VII or ADA federal lawsuit; FMLA and FLSA have no exhaustion requirement

## Title VII of the Civil Rights Act (1964)

**Source:** https://www.eeoc.gov/statutes/title-vii-civil-rights-act-1964

### Coverage
- Prohibits discrimination based on **race, color, religion, sex, national origin**
- Applies to **employers with 15+ employees** in 20+ calendar weeks in current or prior year
- Covers: private employers, state/local governments, employment agencies, labor organizations, federal government
- Excludes: U.S. military, Indian tribes, private membership clubs, foreign operations where compliance would violate foreign law

### Key Provisions

**Section 703 — Unlawful Employment Practices**
- Prohibits discriminatory hiring, firing, compensation, terms, classification of employees
- **Disparate impact:** Neutral policy that disproportionately harms a protected class is unlawful unless job-related and consistent with business necessity
- **BFOQ exception:** Religion, sex, or national origin may be used where "reasonably necessary to normal operation" of the business

**Sex includes:**
- Pregnancy (Pregnancy Discrimination Act amendment)
- Sexual orientation and gender identity (*Bostock v. Clayton County*, 2020 SCOTUS)

**Section 704 — Anti-retaliation**
- Prohibits adverse action against employees who oppose discriminatory practices or participate in EEOC proceedings

**Section 706 — Administrative Exhaustion**
- Must file EEOC charge before federal lawsuit
- Filing deadline: **180 days** (or 300 days in states with parallel state agency)

**Civil Rights Act of 1991 amendments:** Added compensatory and punitive damages; Lilly Ledbetter Fair Pay Act of 2009 extended filing deadlines for pay discrimination

### Damages (capped by employer size)
| Employer Size | Cap |
|--------------|-----|
| 15-100 employees | $50,000 |
| 101-200 employees | $100,000 |
| 201-500 employees | $200,000 |
| 500+ employees | $300,000 |

Plus: Back pay, front pay (not capped), costs, attorney fees

### Common Questions
- **Can we have gender-differentiated dress codes?** Possibly yes, if not unduly burdensome
- **Is a neutral hiring test discriminatory?** Yes, if it produces disparate impact and lacks business justification (validate and document)
- **Does mandatory arbitration waive Title VII rights?** Generally no, but complex; EEOC can still pursue
- **Can a religious employer prefer employees of the same faith?** Yes, under BFOQ exception
- **Who is a "supervisor" for harassment liability purposes?** An employee empowered by employer to take tangible employment action (*Vance v. Ball State*, 2013)

### Enforcement: EEOC
https://www.eeoc.gov/guidance — Investigates charges, attempts conciliation, may sue in federal court

## Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA, 1938)

**Source:** https://www.dol.gov/agencies/whd/flsa

### Coverage
- Establishes **minimum wage, overtime, recordkeeping, child labor** standards
- **Enterprise coverage:** Businesses with $500,000+ annual sales or involved in interstate commerce
- **Individual coverage:** Any employee whose work regularly involves interstate commerce
- Applies to private sector and federal, state, local governments

### Key Provisions

**Minimum Wage:** $7.25/hour (federal floor; higher state/city rates apply)

**Overtime:** 1.5x regular rate for hours over 40 in a workweek; no daily overtime under federal law

**Exempt Employee Categories (White Collar Exemptions, 29 CFR Part 541):**
Must satisfy BOTH a duties test AND salary level test:
- Salary level: $684/week minimum (as of 2025, after Nov. 2024 court vacatur of 2024 rule)
- Highly compensated employees: $107,432/year threshold
- Categories: Executive, Administrative, Professional, Outside Sales, Computer employees

**Independent Contractor Classification (2024 rule, eff. March 11, 2024):**
Six-factor economic reality test; opportunity for profit or loss is significant factor

**Tip credit:** Employers may pay tipped employees $2.13/hour if tips bring total to minimum wage

**Child Labor:** Youth employment restrictions by age and job type

**Recordkeeping:** Employers must maintain time and pay records; must post FLSA poster

**Civil Penalties (2025 rates):**
- Child labor violations: up to $16,035/violation
- Repeated/willful minimum wage/overtime violations: up to $2,515/violation

### Statute of Limitations
2 years (3 years for willful violations)

### Common Questions
- **Is my employee exempt from overtime?** Apply BOTH duties test AND salary level test
- **Can I classify this worker as an independent contractor?** Apply six-factor economic reality test
- **What is "hours worked" for remote workers?** All time employee is "suffered or permitted" to work, including unauthorized overtime
- **Can I pay interns nothing?** Only if meeting DOL's seven-factor "primary beneficiary" test for unpaid internships
- **Do I need to track time for exempt employees?** No mandatory tracking, but best practice for compliance

### Enforcement: DOL Wage and Hour Division
Private suit within 2-3 years; WHD may recover back wages

## Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA, 1990)

**Source:** https://www.ada.gov/law-and-regs/ada/

### Coverage
- **Title I (Employment):** Employers with **15+ employees**
- **Title II (Public entities):** All state and local government programs regardless of size
- **Title III (Public accommodations):** Private businesses open to public (hotels, restaurants, retail)
- **Title IV (Telecommunications):** Requires relay services

### Definition of "Disability" (as amended by ADAAA 2008)
1. A physical or mental impairment that **substantially limits** one or more major life activities
2. A **record** of such impairment
3. **Regarded as** having such impairment

**Key ADAAA 2008 changes** (overturned restrictive SCOTUS interpretations):
- Mitigating measures (medication, devices) are NOT considered when assessing disability (except ordinary eyeglasses)
- Episodic conditions (epilepsy, cancer in remission) count as disabilities when active
- "Disability" must be construed **broadly**

**Major life activities include:** Walking, seeing, hearing, breathing, learning, working, immune system function, cell growth, neurological and reproductive functions

### Key Provisions

**Qualified Individual:** Must be able to perform essential job functions with or without reasonable accommodation

**Reasonable Accommodation:** Employers must provide accommodation unless it causes "undue hardship" (significant difficulty or expense relative to employer's resources)

**Interactive Process:** Employer and employee must engage in good-faith dialogue about accommodation

**Direct Threat Defense:** Employer may exclude individual who poses direct threat to health/safety if no accommodation eliminates risk

**Web Accessibility:** DOJ issued final rule (2024) requiring WCAG 2.1 Level AA for state/local government websites (Title II)

### Common Questions
- **Does anxiety or depression qualify?** Often yes, under the broad ADAAA standard
- **Can I ask about disability in an interview?** No; may only ask whether candidate can perform essential job functions
- **What is a reasonable accommodation?** Modified schedule, remote work, assistive technology, reassignment — evaluated case-by-case based on employer resources
- **Can I fire someone for excessive absences?** Only after exhausting accommodation options including modified leave
- **Do websites need to be ADA accessible?** Yes for Title III entities; WCAG 2.1 Level AA is the current standard

### Enforcement
- Title I: EEOC
- Titles II and III: DOJ Civil Rights Division

## Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA, 1993)

**Source:** https://www.dol.gov/agencies/whd/fmla (updated January 26, 2026)

### Coverage

**Covered Employers:**
- Private sector with **50+ employees** in 20+ workweeks (current or prior year)
- All public agencies regardless of size
- Local educational agencies regardless of size

**Eligible Employees must:**
- Have worked for employer for at least **12 months**
- Have at least **1,250 hours of service** in the prior 12 months
- Work at location where employer has 50+ employees within 75 miles

### Key Provisions

**Leave Entitlement:** Up to **12 workweeks** unpaid, job-protected leave per 12-month period

**Qualifying Reasons:**
- Birth/adoption/foster placement of child (within first year)
- Serious health condition of the employee
- Serious health condition of spouse, child, or parent
- Qualifying military exigency (family member on covered active duty)

**Military Caregiver Leave:** Up to **26 workweeks** per 12-month period for covered servicemember or veteran with serious injury/illness

**Benefits During Leave:** Group health insurance maintained under same terms

**Job Restoration:** Must be restored to same or equivalent position upon return

**Intermittent Leave:** May be taken in blocks, single days, or hourly increments when medically necessary

**Substitution:** Employers may require (or employee may elect) concurrent use of accrued paid leave

**Notice Requirements:**
- Employee: 30 days advance notice when foreseeable
- Employer: must notify employee of FMLA designation within 5 business days of receiving notice

**Medical Certification:** Employers may require from a healthcare provider

### Statute of Limitations
2 years (3 years for willful violations)

### Common Questions
- **Does my 40-person company have to comply?** No; federal FMLA requires 50+ employees. Check your state law — many states have lower thresholds
- **Can we fire someone on FMLA leave?** Only for legitimate, non-FMLA-related reasons applied uniformly
- **Does FMLA cover mental health conditions?** Yes, if meeting "serious health condition" definition
- **Can I require an employee to use PTO during FMLA?** Yes, employers may require concurrent use
- **Does FMLA cover same-sex spouses?** Yes, based on state of celebration of marriage
- **What counts as a "serious health condition"?** Inpatient care, or continuing treatment by healthcare provider — not ordinary illness (cold, flu)

## Compliance Checklist by Employer Size

### 1-14 Employees
- [ ] FLSA compliance (minimum wage, overtime, child labor, recordkeeping)
- [ ] I-9 employment eligibility verification
- [ ] OSHA general duty clause
- [ ] State law equivalents of Title VII, ADA often apply at lower thresholds

### 15-49 Employees
- [ ] All of the above, PLUS:
- [ ] Title VII (anti-discrimination, anti-harassment, anti-retaliation)
- [ ] ADA (disability accommodation, hiring practices)
- [ ] ADEA (age discrimination — applies to 20+ employees)

### 50+ Employees
- [ ] All of the above, PLUS:
- [ ] FMLA (12 weeks unpaid leave, job protection)
- [ ] ACA employer shared responsibility (applicable large employer reporting)
- [ ] VEVRA / Section 503 (federal contractors only — veterans and disability affirmative action)

### 100+ Employees
- [ ] All of the above, PLUS:
- [ ] EEO-1 reporting (annual workforce demographic report to EEOC)
- [ ] WARN Act (60-day notice for mass layoffs of 50+ employees)
